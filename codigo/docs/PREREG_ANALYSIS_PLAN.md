# 1. Overview

This document specifies the pre-registered empirical strategy for the SONAR experiment.

The goal is to study how initial descriptive norms affect the evolution of reporting behavior in sequential settings, with particular focus on:

- maximum reporting behavior (reporting 6),
- dynamic trajectories across ordered series,
- magnitude of misreporting.

All confirmatory analyses are defined ex ante. Exploratory analyses are clearly separated.

---

# 2. Sample Definition

## 2.1 Main sample

The main analysis uses the first:

> **N = 6000 valid completed sessions**

A session is considered valid if:

- consent is accepted,
- first draw is observed,
- final report is submitted,
- no technical corruption is detected,
- no duplicate invalid participation,
- no critical fraud flag.

## 2.2 Exclusions

The following observations are excluded from confirmatory analysis:

- missing report
- missing true first result
- inconsistent state transitions
- duplicate completed bracelet
- corrupted sessions
- critical fraud flags

All exclusions are pre-specified and logged.

---

# 3. Experimental Design

## 3.1 Treatments

Participants are assigned to one of three conditions:

- `control` -> 10%
- `seed_17` -> 45%
- `seed_83` -> 45%

## 3.2 Interpretation

- `seed_17`: low norm (17/100 reported 6)
- `seed_83`: high norm (83/100 reported 6)
- `control`: no social information

## 3.3 Series Structure

- participants are embedded in sequential series
- maximum series length: 250
- social information based on last 100 reports
- series are mirrored across treatments (shared underlying stochastic sequence)

## 3.4 Stochastic Process

The true outcome is generated by a balanced deck:

- preserves randomness at participant level
- constrains local distribution
- ensures comparability across mirrored series

---

# 4. Unit of Analysis

## 4.1 Primary unit

`(root_id, position_index)`

Comparisons are made across treatments at identical positions within mirrored series.

## 4.2 Secondary unit

Individual session-level observations.

---

# 5. Outcomes

## 5.1 Primary outcome

`reported_6 = 1{reported_value = 6}`

## 5.2 Secondary outcomes

- `is_honest = 1{reported_value = true_first_result}`
- `lie_amount = reported_value - true_first_result`
- `reported_5 = 1{reported_value = 5}`
- `reported_high = 1{reported_value >= 5}`

## 5.3 Opportunity-adjusted variables

- `opportunity_to_lie = 6 - true_first_result`
- `relative_lie = lie_amount / opportunity_to_lie` (if > 0)

---

# 6. Hypotheses

## H1 - Main effect

> Series with high initial norm (`seed_83`) exhibit higher probability of reporting 6 than `seed_17`.

## H2 - Early effect

> The difference between treatments emerges in early positions (1-100).

## H3 - Persistence

> The difference persists in later positions (101-250).

## H4 - Control benchmark

> Control lies between seed_17 and seed_83 (descriptive).

## H5 - Magnitude

> High norm increases not only probability of lying but magnitude of misreporting.

---

# 7. Estimation Strategy

## 7.1 Difference in means

Primary estimand:

`E[reported_6 | seed_83] - E[reported_6 | seed_17]`

Estimated for:

- positions 1-250
- positions 1-100
- positions 101-250

## 7.2 Regression specification

Baseline model:

`Y_{rjt} = alpha_r + f(t) + beta Treatment_j + epsilon`

Where:

- `alpha_r`: root fixed effects
- `f(t)`: flexible function of position
- `beta`: treatment effect

## 7.3 Extended model

`Y_{rjt} = alpha_r + f(t) + beta Treatment + g(t) x Treatment + epsilon`

## 7.4 Conditioning on true outcome

All analyses are repeated controlling for:

`true_first_result`

## 7.5 Restricted samples

Additional robustness:

- `true_first_result <= 4`
- `true_first_result <= 3`

---

# 8. Inference

- standard errors clustered at root level
- bootstrap by root used if number of clusters is small

# 12. Interpretation Strategy

The study focuses on:

> how norms shift trajectories of reporting behavior, not isolated individual decisions.

We interpret results as:

- conditional behavioral responses,
- not fixed moral types.

---

# 13. Limitations

- balanced deck is not IID
- single observation per participant
- contextual variation uncontrolled
- external validity context-dependent
